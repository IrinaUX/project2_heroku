Introduction to the project

The catastrophic Hurricane Laura made landfall on August 27th, 2020 in Cameron, Louisiana with a maximum wind speed of 150 mph, causing 71 fatalities and over $10 billion (USD) in damage. This hurricane was personal to our team because it had a strong chance of vearing further west towards Houston, and there was a mandadory evactuation of Galveston Island.

After this scare, we decided to form our project around the costliest Atlantic Hurricanes over the last 40 years, and study how wind speed and landfall locations impact damage and cost to the states affected.

Please join us in this review the historical Atlantic hurricanes data.


Damages overview - US by states (1980 - 2019)



The three states that sustained the most damage from hurricanes over the last 40 years in the US are Texas, Florida, and Louisiana.

The latest statistics from NOAA shows total damages from hurricanes and floods in Texas just over $250 billion (USD), over $210 billion (USD) in Florida, and nearly $180 billion (USD) in Louisiana. Mississippi, North Carolina, and New York encountered on average $50 billion (USD) in damages.

You will notice that the Northwest of the US has taken very little damage from hurricanes, while the Southeast has been hit very hard. Hurricanes in the Northeast Pacific almost never hit the US, whereas the ones in the Atlantic basin strike the US mainland just less that twice a year on average. One reason for this is the temperature of the water the hurricanes are formed and move over.

The Gulf Stream provides a source of warm water, above 80 degrees Fahrenheit, which helps maintain a hurricanes strength in the Atlantic. Along the West Coast, ocean temperatures rarely rise above 70 degrees Fahrenheit which do not provide enough thermal energy to sustain a hurricane's strength.

Hurricane Wind Speeds vs. Cost



Hurricane Katrina was an exceptionally large Category 5 hurricane as it approached the Gulf Coast. Category 5 storms have sustained winds in excess of 156 mph.

Katrina’s hurricane-force winds of 160 mph extended 90 miles from the center, and tropical-storm-force winds stretched 200 miles in each direction from its center. Hurricanes are typically no more than 300 miles wide.

Right before landfall, Hurricane Katrina weakened to a Category 3 hurricane with winds as strong as 125 mph.
